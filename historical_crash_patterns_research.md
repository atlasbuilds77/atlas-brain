# Historical Stock Market Crash Patterns and Leading Indicators

## Research Summary: Warning Signs from Three Major Crashes

### 1. 2008 Financial Crisis - Leading Indicators (6-12 Months Before)

**Timeline of Early Warning Signs:**
- **2004-2006**: U.S. homeownership peaked at 69.2% (2004)
- **Early 2006**: Home prices began to decline nationwide
- **May 2006**: JPMorgan warned clients of housing downturn, especially in subprime
- **August 2006**: Yield curve inverted (recession signal within 1-2 years)
- **November 2006**: UBS warned of "an impending crisis in the U.S. housing market"
- **2006**: One-third of all mortgages were subprime or no-documentation loans (17% of home purchases)
- **2006**: Mortgage originations to investors rose from 25% (2000) to 45% in bubble states (AZ, CA, FL, NV)

**Specific Data Points:**
- **Subprime lending**: New Century Financial made nearly $60 billion in loans in 2006, filed bankruptcy in 2007
- **Yield curve inversion**: Two-year Treasury bill yield exceeded seven-year note yield (December 2005-January 2006)
- **Housing market**: Prices peaked in early 2006, began declining in 2006-2007
- **Federal Reserve actions**: Raised federal funds rate from 1% (2003) to 5.25% (2006), where it remained until August 2007

**Key Warning Signs:**
1. Housing price declines starting early 2006
2. Yield curve inversion (August 2006)
3. Rising mortgage defaults (reached seven-year high in February 2007)
4. Subprime lender bankruptcies (25+ lenders failed Feb-Mar 2007)
5. Bear Stearns hedge fund redemptions stopped (June 2007)
6. Interbank market freezing (August 2007)

### 2. 2000 Dot-Com Crash - Early Warnings

**Timeline of Warning Signs:**
- **1998-1999**: Low interest rates facilitated startup boom
- **1999**: Nasdaq Composite P/E ratio surpassed 90, reached ~200 at peak
- **1999**: Only 14% of IPO tech firms were profitable at the peak
- **1999**: Nasdaq-100 trading at forward P/E ratio of 60.1x
- **October 1999**: Nasdaq fell 10% in a single session (first stress sign)
- **1999**: Shares of Qualcomm rose 2,619%, 12 other large-cap stocks rose over 1,000%
- **2000**: Average price-to-sales (P/S) ratio of companies going public: 48.9

**Specific Data Points:**
- **Valuation extremes**: Cisco had peak P/E ratio of 472 in 1999
- **Revenue vs. losses**: Example company - Revenue grew from $7M (1998) to $151M (1999) to $166M (2000), but losses exploded from -$28M to -$128M to -$186M
- **Burn rates**: Average burn rates > $10 million/month for top-tier startups
- **Market concentration**: Only handful of tech stocks drove market gains
- **Speculation**: Investors borrowed heavily on margin

**Key Warning Signs:**
1. Extreme P/E ratios (Nasdaq P/E > 90, individual stocks > 400)
2. Low profitability (only 14% of tech IPOs profitable)
3. High burn rates with no path to profitability
4. Parabolic chart patterns with weakening momentum
5. Excessive speculation and margin debt
6. "Dumb money" flowing into Nasdaq

### 3. 1929 Crash Indicators

**Timeline of Warning Signs:**
- **1927**: Business inventories began rising as consumer spending declined
- **July 1928**: Federal Reserve raised interest rates to curb speculation
- **March 1929**: Small crash occurred after Fed warned of excessive speculation
- **August 1929**: Fed raised interest rates again to slow stock prices
- **Summer 1929**: Economy showed signs of cooling with declining production and rising unemployment

**Specific Data Points:**
- **Margin debt**: Investors buying stocks with only 10% down payment
- **Market psychology**: General consensus that stock price rise was unsustainable
- **Economic indicators**: Declining production, rising unemployment, struggling agricultural sector
- **Federal Reserve actions**: Public campaign starting 1928 to slow runaway stock prices
- **Expert warnings**: Roger Babson predicted crash in September 1929

**Key Warning Signs:**
1. Excessive margin buying (10% down payments)
2. Federal Reserve publicly warning about speculation
3. Rising interest rates to curb market
4. Economic cooling (declining production, rising unemployment)
5. Expert predictions of unsustainable valuations
6. Public awareness that price rise couldn't continue indefinitely

### 4. Common Patterns Across All Three Crashes

**1. Excessive Leverage/Debt:**
- 1929: Margin buying with 10% down payments
- 2000: Heavy margin debt for tech stock speculation
- 2008: Mortgage debt, CDOs, bank leverage (30-40x)

**2. Valuation Extremes:**
- 1929: Stock prices wildly inflated vs. economic fundamentals
- 2000: P/E ratios > 90 for Nasdaq, individual stocks > 400
- 2008: Housing prices disconnected from income/affordability

**3. Speculative Mania:**
- 1929: "Everyone" investing in stocks
- 2000: Dot-com IPOs with no profits
- 2008: Housing speculation, "flipping" homes

**4. Credit/Liquidity Changes:**
- 1929: Fed raising rates to curb speculation
- 2000: Low rates initially, then tightening
- 2008: Rate hikes from 2004-2006, credit freezing

**5. Warning from Authorities:**
- 1929: Fed public warnings about speculation
- 2000: Some economists warning about valuations
- 2008: Some banks/institutions warning about housing

**6. Economic Disconnects:**
- 1929: Stock prices vs. declining production
- 2000: Tech valuations vs. profitability
- 2008: Housing prices vs. income/affordability

**7. New Financial Instruments:**
- 1929: Margin accounts enabling speculation
- 2000: Tech stock options, IPO frenzy
- 2008: Mortgage-backed securities, CDOs

### 5. Checklist of Warning Signs for Future Crashes

**Valuation Metrics:**
- [ ] P/E ratios significantly above historical averages (>25 for S&P 500)
- [ ] Price-to-sales ratios extreme (>10 for broad market)
- [ ] CAPE ratio (Shiller P/E) > 30
- [ ] Asset prices disconnected from fundamentals

**Debt/Leverage Indicators:**
- [ ] Margin debt at record highs
- [ ] Household debt-to-income ratios elevated
- [ ] Corporate debt levels concerning
- [ ] Bank leverage ratios excessive

**Market Behavior:**
- [ ] Parabolic price increases
- [ ] Narrow leadership (few stocks driving gains)
- [ ] Excessive IPO activity with poor fundamentals
- [ ] "Dumb money" flooding into market

**Economic Indicators:**
- [ ] Yield curve inversion
- [ ] Declining leading economic indicators
- [ ] Credit spreads widening
- [ ] Liquidity drying up

**Psychological/Sentiment:**
- [ ] "This time is different" mentality
- [ ] Widespread belief prices can only go up
- [ ] Dismissal of traditional valuation metrics
- [ ] Mainstream media celebrating gains

**Regulatory/Policy:**
- [ ] Central banks warning about speculation
- [ ] Interest rate hikes to cool markets
- [ ] Regulatory changes enabling risk-taking
- [ ] Deteriorating credit standards

**Specific Sector Overheating:**
- [ ] One sector dominating market gains
- [ ] Sector valuations extreme vs. history
- [ ] New paradigm thinking about specific sector
- [ ] Herd behavior into "hot" sector

### 6. Key Data Points to Monitor

1. **Valuation ratios** (P/E, P/S, CAPE) vs. historical norms
2. **Margin debt** as percentage of market capitalization
3. **Household debt** to disposable income ratio
4. **Corporate debt** to EBITDA ratios
5. **Yield curve** (2yr-10yr spread)
6. **Credit spreads** (high yield vs. treasury)
7. **IPO activity** (volume and quality)
8. **Market breadth** (advance-decline line)
9. **Sentiment indicators** (put/call ratio, VIX)
10. **Federal Reserve** statements and actions

### 7. Conclusion

Historical analysis reveals consistent patterns preceding major crashes:
- **Excessive optimism** leading to valuation extremes
- **Leverage amplification** of gains and subsequent losses
- **New financial innovations** enabling speculation
- **Regulatory/policy responses** that are too late or inadequate
- **Economic disconnects** between asset prices and fundamentals

The most reliable warning signs appear to be combinations of extreme valuations, high leverage, deteriorating credit conditions, and changing monetary policy. No single indicator is perfect, but clusters of these warning signs have preceded all major historical crashes.

**Most Critical Early Warning Signs:**
1. Yield curve inversion (6-18 months before recession)
2. Extreme valuation metrics (P/E, CAPE)
3. Rapid credit expansion followed by tightening
4. Deteriorating market breadth
5. Rising margin debt coupled with parabolic price moves
