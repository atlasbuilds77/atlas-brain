## 1. Position Sizing Formulas

### Kelly Criterion
The Kelly Criterion is a mathematical formula developed by John L. Kelly Jr. at Bell Labs in 1956 to determine optimal position sizing.

**Formula:**
```
K% = W - [(1 - W) / R]
```
Where:
- `K%` = Kelly percentage (optimal fraction of capital to allocate)
- `W` = Win probability (probability of a positive return)
- `R` = Win/loss ratio (average gain ÷ average loss)

**Practical Application:**
1. Calculate win probability (W) from historical trades
2. Calculate win/loss ratio (R) from average gains vs average losses
3. Apply formula to get optimal allocation percentage

**Key Considerations:**
- Full Kelly is aggressive - most traders use Half Kelly or Quarter Kelly for safety
- Recommended: 0.10x-0.15x of Kelly-optimal size for conservative approach
- Never allocate more than 20-25% of capital to any single position
- Assumes historical performance will continue (may not always be accurate)

### Other Position Sizing Methods
- **Optimal F**: Similar to Kelly but uses different risk parameters
- **CPPI (Constant Proportion Portfolio Insurance)**: Dynamic allocation based on cushion between current value and floor
- **TIPP (Time-Invariant Portfolio Protection)**: Modified CPPI with time-invariant characteristics

## 2. Risk Management Rules for Day Traders

### The 1-2% Rule
The most fundamental risk management rule for day traders:

**1% Rule:** Risk no more than 1% of total trading capital on any single trade
**2% Rule:** Alternative approach allowing up to 2% risk per trade

**Calculation:**
```
Maximum Risk per Trade = Account Size × Risk Percentage
Position Size = Maximum Risk ÷ Stop Loss Distance
```

**Example:**
- $50,000 account using 2% rule
- Maximum risk = $50,000 × 0.02 = $1,000
- Stock price: $170, Stop loss: $15
- Position size = $1,000 ÷ $15 = 67 shares

### Daily Loss Limits
- Set maximum daily loss limit (typically 2-5% of account)
- Stop trading for the day when limit is reached
- Helps prevent emotional "revenge trading"

### Practical Implementation Guidelines:
1. **Beginners:** 0.5% to 1% risk per trade
2. **Experienced:** 1% to 2% risk per trade  
3. **Professionals:** May use 2-3% but rarely exceed 3%
4. **Daily maximum:** 2-5% of account value

## 3. Trading Psychology Best Practices

### Emotional Control
**Common Emotional Triggers:**
- **Fear:** Causes missed opportunities, premature exits
- **Greed:** Leads to overleveraging, holding winners too long
- **Anxiety:** Impacts execution speed and decision quality
- **Hope:** Prevents cutting losses, leads to "hope trading"

### Discipline Framework
1. **Trading Plan:** Create detailed plan with predefined:
   - Entry/exit points
   - Position sizes
   - Risk management rules
   - Market conditions for trading

2. **Trading Journal:** Essential for psychological improvement
   - Record all trades with reasoning
   - Note emotional state during each trade
   - Review patterns in emotional behavior
   - Identify psychological weaknesses

3. **Routine Development:**
   - Pre-market preparation routine
   - Post-market review routine
   - Regular self-reflection sessions
   - Visualization techniques for success

### Mindset Development
- **Accept losses** as part of trading
- **Focus on process** over outcomes
- **Develop patience** and wait for high-probability setups
- **Maintain objectivity** by removing ego from decisions
- **Practice emotional detachment** from individual trades

## 4. Drawdown Management

### Understanding Drawdown
**Maximum Drawdown (MDD):** The largest peak-to-trough decline in portfolio value over a specific period.

**Types of Drawdown:**
1. **Absolute Drawdown:** Loss from initial deposit
2. **Maximum Drawdown:** Worst loss from any peak
3. **Relative Drawdown:** Loss relative to recent high

### Drawdown Management Strategies

**Threshold-Based Approach:**
- Set specific equity thresholds (5%, 10%, 15%)
- Reduce position sizes when thresholds are breached
- Consider trading suspension at maximum thresholds

**Recommended Limits:**
- **Good maximum drawdown:** Less than 25%
- **Conservative threshold:** 10% maximum drawdown
- **Aggressive threshold:** 15-20% maximum drawdown

**Recovery Considerations:**
- **Recovery Factor:** Total net profit ÷ Maximum drawdown
- Higher recovery factor indicates better risk-adjusted returns
- Psychological recovery is as important as financial recovery

### Psychological Impact of Drawdowns
- Drawdowns create psychological pressure and overwhelm
- Recovery requires both financial and emotional resilience
- The larger the drawdown, the harder the psychological recovery
- Implement mental breaks during significant drawdowns

## 5. When to Cut Losses vs Hold

### Stop Loss Strategies

**Fixed Stop Loss:**
- Set at predetermined price level
- Based on technical analysis or volatility
- Never move stop loss in wrong direction (to avoid loss)

**Trailing Stop Loss:**
- Dynamic stop that follows price upward
- Never lowered, only increased
- Locks in profits while allowing winners to run

**Mental Stops:**
- For disciplined traders only
- Requires strong emotional control
- Risk of psychological stop-loss violation

### Decision Framework: Cut Losses vs Hold

**Cut Losses When:**
1. **Trade thesis is invalidated:** Original reason for entry no longer valid
2. **Technical breakdown:** Key support levels broken with conviction
3. **Time stop reached:** Expected move hasn't materialized within timeframe
4. **Risk limit hit:** Position reaches maximum allowable loss
5. **Better opportunity exists:** Capital could be deployed more effectively

**Consider Holding When:**
1. **Thesis remains intact:** Original reasons for trade still valid
2. **Within normal volatility:** Price action is within expected range
3. **Position size appropriate:** Loss is manageable within risk parameters
4. **Timeframe alignment:** Still within expected holding period
5. **No better alternatives:** No superior risk-reward opportunities available

### Psychological Barriers to Cutting Losses

**Disposition Effect:** Tendency to hold losing investments and sell winners too soon

**Overcoming Psychological Barriers:**
1. **Automate exits:** Use stop-loss orders to remove emotion
2. **Pre-commitment:** Decide exit points before entering trade
3. **Focus on process:** Evaluate trade quality, not outcome
4. **Accept imperfection:** No system wins 100% of trades
5. **Review objectively:** Analyze losing trades without emotion

## Key Takeaways

### Position Sizing
- Use Kelly Criterion or fractional Kelly for optimal sizing
- Never risk more than 1-2% per trade
- Adjust position size based on stop loss distance

### Risk Management
- Implement daily loss limits
- Use stop losses consistently
- Diversify across markets and timeframes

### Psychology
- Maintain trading journal for emotional awareness
- Develop pre- and post-market routines
- Focus on process over individual trade outcomes

### Drawdown Management
- Set maximum drawdown thresholds
- Reduce position sizes during drawdowns
- Allow psychological recovery time

### Loss Management
- Cut losses quickly when thesis invalidates
- Let winners run with trailing stops
- Remove emotion through automation and discipline
